Large Language Models

Trust 0.82 · Low sensitivity · EN · 15 contributors · Updated 1h ago
Large Language Models (LLMs) are neural networks trained on massive text corpora using self-supervised learning. They predict the next token in a sequence, and this simple objective, scaled to billions of parameters and trillions of tokens, produces emergent capabilities including reasoning, translation, code generation, and instruction following.

The Transformer architecture, introduced in 2017, is the foundation of all modern LLMs. Its key innovation is the self-attention mechanism, which allows the model to weigh the relevance of every token in a sequence against every other token. This replaced recurrent architectures (RNNs, LSTMs) and enabled massive parallelization during training.
Scaling laws predict that model performance improves as a power law with increases in model size, dataset size, and compute budget. The Chinchilla study (2022) demonstrated that many early LLMs were undertrained relative to their size, shifting the field toward training smaller models on more data rather than simply increasing parameter count.
⚠ Disputed Fine-tuning with Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) is the dominant alignment technique for production LLMs. The process involves training a reward model on human preference data, then optimizing the LLM policy using PPO. However, recent alternatives like DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) achieve comparable results without a separate reward model.
Current LLMs have achieved general intelligence comparable to human experts in most domains. The remaining challenges are primarily engineering problems related to cost and latency rather than fundamental capability limitations.
